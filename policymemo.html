Economic Policy Memo

Unlocking Household Savings to Close the UK's Productivity Gap

Executive Summary

The UK government should introduce auto-enrolment into Stocks and Shares ISAs (S&S), paired with a UK Productive Finance Fund, to address both the household wealth gap and chronic underinvestment in productive capital. Britain’s productivity has averaged just 0.5% annual growth since 2008, near the bottom of OECD rankings, driven by broad-based underinvestment. A crucial overlooked dimension is where ordinary people’s savings sit: 94% of UK adults hold no S&S ISA, leaving household wealth in low-return cash vehicles that fund little productive activity. Auto-enrolment and a productive finance mandate would channel household savings toward the firms and regions that need capital most, while building wealth for those currently excluded.

Background

Productivity, output per hour worked, is the primary driver of wages and living standards. An S&S ISA allows UK residents to invest up to £20,000 annually in stocks, bonds, and funds, with all returns tax-free. Unlike a Cash ISA, which functions like a savings account, an S&S ISA exposes savings to capital markets, offering higher long-term returns in exchange for some investment risk.

Chronic Underinvestment Has Stalled UK Productivity

UK productivity has barely grown since the financial crisis. Output per hour worked has risen only around 5% since 2008, against a pre-crisis trend that would have delivered nearly 40% growth. Annual productivity growth averaged just 0.5% from 2010 to 2022, placing the UK well behind France, Germany, and the United States (van Ark and O'Mahony, 2024). If this continues, the UK cannot sustain current living standards.

The root cause is broad-based underinvestment. Total investment as a share of GDP has declined since the 1980s across both public and private capital. Decades of neglect in skills and training have trapped many firms in a low-skill, low-wage, low-productivity cycle, while austerity from 2010 further weakened public investment. The consequences are uneven: Wales, the West and East Midlands, and the North East show productivity levels well below London. What this diagnosis consistently overlooks is household savings — the vast majority of which sit in low-return cash vehicles generating little productive investment.

S&S ISAs Work But Participation Is Too Narrow

S&S ISAs are effective wealth-building tools, but their reach is far too narrow. Long-run UK equity returns have historically averaged 5–7% annually in real terms – well above cash savings (Dimson, Marsh and Staunton, 2023). At the household level, S&S ISA holders accumulate approximately 27% more wealth than similar non-holders even after controlling for income, age, and initial wealth (Rushworth, 2026). Engaging with investment markets even briefly generates lasting changes in financial behaviour that persist beyond account closure.

Yet only 6% of UK adults held an S&S ISA by 2022, against 12% for Cash ISAs. Participation is deeply skewed: 60% of those earning over £150,000 maximise their annual allowance, while lower earners participate far less (Chart 5). The Resolution Foundation estimates the £6.7 billion annual foregone tax revenue flows disproportionately to higher earners (Broome, 2025) – consistent with wider evidence that tax-efficient savings vehicles reward those with the greatest capacity to save rather than those who need it most (Gregory, Lymer and Rowlingson, 2022). A policy designed to democratise wealth is functioning as a subsidy for the already wealthy.

The Barrier Is Entry, Not The Product

S&S ISAs are not inherently elitist. The barrier is getting people through the door. Behavioural economics shows that individuals who engage with investment markets, even modestly, develop stronger financial habits and long-term wealth resilience (Thaler and Benartzi, 2004). Asset ownership generates not just financial returns but behavioural changes that compound over time (Sherraden, 1991). UK evidence shows that less-educated individuals benefit more from S&S ISA adoption than graduates – the product does not penalise the less advantaged, the entry barrier does (Rushworth, 2026).

This connects directly to productivity. The households most excluded from capital markets — lower-income, less-educated, in post-industrial regions — are those where the productivity gap is deepest. Low wages make saving harder, limiting investment participation, concentrating wealth, deepening regional inequality, and sustaining low productivity. Breaking this cycle requires targeting the entry barrier, not expanding relief for those already inside the system.

Recommendations

Two linked recommendations address both the wealth gap and the productive investment shortfall.

  1. Auto-enrol workers into S&S ISAs. Modelled on the 2012 pension auto-enrolment reforms, which transformed retirement saving by making participation the default, workers should be automatically enrolled at £50 per month, with a simple opt-out. HMRC and employers already have the infrastructure. The target should be raising participation from 6% to 30% within a decade. Getting people invested once is sufficient to generate lasting benefits.
  2. Create a UK Productive Finance ISA (PF-ISA). A ring-fenced, ISA-eligible fund investing in mid-tier UK firms – those most in need of capital to close the productivity gap (van Ark and O'Mahony, 2024). The government should co-invest 25p per £1 for first-time investors earning below median income, funded by redirecting a portion of the existing £6.7bn tax expenditure away from high-earner Cash ISA relief. Australia's superannuation system and France's Plan d'Épargne en Actions show that channelling retail savings into domestic productive investment at scale is achievable.

Conclusion

Britain’s productivity crisis is typically framed as a failure of government and corporate investment. That framing is incomplete. Ordinary people’s savings are part of the investment story, and right now they are in the wrong place. Auto-enrolment works. ISAs work. What is missing is the will to combine them and direct the result toward productive ends. The Autumn Budget 2025 has already signalled intent by cutting Cash ISA limits while preserving the S&S allowance. The architecture exists. Acting now would give ordinary people a stake in the economy and give the economy the capital it needs to grow.
